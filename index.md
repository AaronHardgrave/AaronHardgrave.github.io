## Research & Publications

### Dissertation Research: Functional Morphology of the Eastern Newt

![Eastern newt volume renderings showing osteological elements (left) and diceCT soft tissue capture (right)](images/overview.jpg)
*Eastern newt volume renderings: Osteological elements (left) and diceCT soft tissue capture (right)*

**How does a newt's body shape change as it moves between water and land?**

Eastern newts (*Notophthalmus viridescens*) are remarkable amphibians that live in water as juveniles, move onto land as young adults, and then return to water to reproduce. This unique life cycle raises an intriguing question: does the newt's skeleton change shape to adapt to these different environments?

My research uses advanced 3D imaging technology (micro-CT scanning) to capture detailed pictures of newt skeletons across their different life stages. I then apply geometric morphometricsâ€”a technique that analyzes and compares shapes mathematicallyâ€”to measure exactly how the vertebral column, limbs, and muscles differ between aquatic and terrestrial newts.

Understanding how form relates to function in newts helps us understand broader principles of evolution and adaptation. It also provides insights into biomechanical constraintsâ€”the physical trade-offs animals face when their bodies must work in multiple environments.

## Teaching

### Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy centers on **functional understanding over memorization**. I believe anatomy students should develop the ability to visualize structures and infer their function based on anatomical relationships, preparing them to think anatomically in clinical practice rather than simply recall facts for examinations.
